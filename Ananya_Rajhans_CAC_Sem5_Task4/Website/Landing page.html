The impact of globalization on the gender pay gap is complex and multifaceted. On one hand, globalization has the potential to contribute to closing the gender pay gap by creating more opportunities for women in the workforce. Increased global connectivity and access to information have facilitated a broader understanding of gender equality, encouraging companies and societies to address disparities. On the other hand, globalization can also widen the gender pay gap. Economic globalization sometimes leads to the outsourcing of jobs, and women in certain sectors may be disproportionately affected, facing challenges such as lower wages and poorer working conditions. Additionally, the globalized economy may reinforce traditional gender roles, particularly in industries that are more resistant to change.

In the early to mid-20th century, the gender wage gap was deeply entrenched in societies across the globe. There were relatively few women in the workforce, primarily due to cultural and societal norms that dictated that a woman's primary role was within the home. Those who did enter the workforce often faced barriers in terms of educational opportunities. The prevailing belief was that women were inherently less capable than men, both intellectually and physically, reinforcing stereotypes that limited their access to education and professional opportunities. The gender wage gap refers to the difference between the average earnings of men and women in the workforce. It is typically expressed as a percentage of men's earnings, highlighting the disparity in income between genders. This gap exists across various industries, occupations, and educational levels and is influenced by a combination of factors such as discrimination, occupational segregation, differences in work experience, and societal expectations. The gender wage gap is a critical issue as it reflects and perpetuates gender inequality, limiting economic opportunities for women and contributing to broader socio-economic disparities.
